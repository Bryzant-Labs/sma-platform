## Abstract

This report consolidates findings from a multi-LLM research sprint targeting three key areas
of SMA biology: (1) non-canonical SMN protein interactions with emphasis on actin dynamics,
(2) intron retention as a disease mechanism, and (3) proprioceptive circuit dysfunction.
The most significant finding is that **SMN directly interacts with Profilin 1 (PFN1) in
motor neurons** (PMID 21920940), placing SMN at the center of the actin dynamics pathway
and strengthening the biological plausibility of our actin pathway convergence hypothesis
(CORO1C-PLS3-CFL2-PFN1). However, critical analysis by GPT-4o rates the CORO1C double-hit
hypothesis as "moderate to low" probability, primarily due to lack of direct experimental
evidence for intron retention and CORO1C's likely status as a downstream passenger rather
than an independent driver.

## 1. SMN Non-Canonical Protein Interactions (Gemini Flash Research)

### Key Finding: SMN-Profilin Axis
SMN interacts directly with:
- **Profilin 1 (PFN1)** — Co-IP, validated in motor neurons (PMID 21920940)
- **Profilin 2 (PFN2)** — Co-IP, Western blot (PMID 16885108)

This places SMN at the hub of actin polymerization control. Both profilins regulate
actin dynamics, and PFN1 mutations cause ALS (familial ALS type 20), creating a
convergence point between SMA and ALS at the actin pathway level.

## 2. Intron Retention as a Disease Mechanism (Gemini Flash Research)

### Known Pathological Intron Retention in Motor Neuron Diseases
| Disease | Gene | Mechanism | Significance |
|---------|------|-----------|--------------|
| ALS | UNC13A | C9orf72-driven IR | Major ALS event |
| ALS | STMN2 | TDP-43 depletion | Readthrough, loss of function |
| ALS | KCNQ2 | TDP-43 depletion | Potassium channel affected |
| SMA | SMN2 | Canonical exon 7 skipping | Primary disease mechanism |
| FTD | MAPT | Tau isoform alteration | Tauopathies |

### Does SMN Loss Cause Widespread Intron Retention?
**Evidence supports this but is not definitive:**
- SMN is essential for snRNP assembly and recycling → general splicing defects expected
- RNA-seq in SMA models shows widespread splicing alterations
- However: distinguishing direct SMN effects from secondary stress effects is challenging
- **Minor spliceosome (U12-dependent) introns may be specifically vulnerable** — these
  represent ~0.5% of introns but are found in essential genes

### Detection Methods for CORO1C Intron Retention
- **RT-PCR**: Primers flanking the 112bp intron — simplest validation
- **IRFinder**: Computational tool specifically for intron retention from RNA-seq
- **SUPPA2**: Differential splicing quantification including PSI values
- **rMATS**: Statistical splicing analysis (recommended by Simon)

### Therapeutic Approaches Beyond ASOs
1. Small molecule splicing modulators (Pladienolide B, E7107)
2. Gene therapy delivering functional splicing factors
3. Targeting RNA-binding proteins (hnRNP A1, Tra2-beta)
4. CRISPR-based editing of splicing regulatory elements

## 3. CORO1C Double-Hit Hypothesis — Critical Analysis (GPT-4o)

### GPT-4o Verdict: "Moderate to Low Probability"

**Strongest Points:**
1. GSE87281 expression data is statistically robust (n=101, FDR=1.5e-71)
2. CORO1C-PLS3 interaction confirmed (STRING 0.818)
3. Actin pathway convergence (CORO1C + PLS3 + CFL2 + PFN1)
4. SMN-PFN1 interaction validated in motor neurons

**Weakest Points:**
1. No direct experimental proof of CORO1C intron retention in SMA
2. Conflicting expression data (GSE69175 shows upregulation, though n=2)
3. Wirth Lab evidence: CORO1C is downstream of PLS3, not independent
4. Zero existing literature connecting CORO1C intron retention to SMN

### Assessment: Driver vs. Passenger
**CORO1C is more likely a passenger gene.** It is downstream of PLS3 in the
actin pathway hierarchy. The actin dynamics disruption is real, but CORO1C
is probably one of many affected proteins, not the key therapeutic target.

### What Would Change This Assessment
1. **RT-PCR showing CORO1C intron retention in SMN-depleted cells** → would raise
   probability significantly
2. **CORO1C rescue experiment** → if overexpressing CORO1C rescues SMA phenotype
   in motor neurons, driver status confirmed
3. **Genetic interaction** → if CORO1C knockdown worsens SMA in animal model,
   independent modifier role established

### Revised Hypothesis
The stronger hypothesis is: **SMN loss → impaired actin dynamics via PFN1/PFN2
pathway disruption → multiple downstream effects including reduced CORO1C, PLS3
modifier effect, and CFL2/actin remodeling deficits.** CORO1C is a marker of
the pathway, not necessarily the intervention point.

## 4. Proprioceptive Synaptic Dysfunction (Gemini Flash Research)

### Key Finding: SMA is a Circuit Disease, Not Just a Motor Neuron Disease

The Mentis lab (Columbia) has extensively documented:
- **Ia afferent synapse loss** on motor neurons precedes or occurs with MN degeneration
- **DRG neuron dysfunction** — altered morphology, reduced excitability
- **Sensory-motor connectivity disruption** — proprioceptive innervation specificity lost
- **NMJ abnormalities** — proprioceptive-innervated NMJs are most vulnerable

### Timeline: Proprioceptive Dysfunction Precedes Motor Neuron Death
This is critical: the circuit fails before neurons die. This means:
1. Motor neuron counting alone is insufficient (validates Simon's critique)
2. Therapeutic window may be larger than thought (circuit rescue before cell death)
3. Electrical stimulation approaches have biological plausibility

### Current View: Cell-Autonomous → Circuit Disease
The field is shifting from "SMA = motor neuron death" to "SMA = sensory-motor
circuit breakdown." Non-cell-autonomous mechanisms (glial, sensory, muscle)
are increasingly recognized.

### Implications for Our Platform
- We need a **proprioceptive circuit module** (currently missing)
- Motor neuron counting data should include methodology quality scores
- Simon's emphasis on proprioception is well-supported by literature
- Spinal cord stimulation (Capogrosso lab) has theoretical basis but NO SMA data

|----------|---------------|---------------|
| Epidural SCS | Pilot studies in SCI, nothing in SMA | No |
| 4-AP (fampridine) | Symptomatic relief in MS, no neuroprotection evidence | No |
| FES | Prevents muscle atrophy, no MN preservation evidence | No |
| tSCS | May improve respiratory function in ALS | No |

### Ion Channel Dysregulation in SMA (Validated)
- Calcium channels: disrupted homeostasis, excitotoxicity
- Potassium channels: altered expression, changed excitability
- BK channels: reduced expression → increased excitability + Ca influx
- Sodium channels: impaired action potential propagation

### 4-AP Reality Check
Our DiffDock finding (4-AP → CORO1C, confidence +0.251) is computationally
interesting but:
- 4-AP has no evidence of neuroprotection at clinical doses
- 4-AP at high doses is neurotoxic
- The binding prediction has not been experimentally validated
- **Assessment: computational curiosity, not a therapeutic lead**

### Vmem Hypothesis
Depolarized resting membrane potential → increased Ca influx → excitotoxicity
is a plausible mechanism. Our estimate of 40% rescuable MNs via bioelectric
intervention is speculative but has theoretical grounding.

## 13. "Translational Desert" Hypothesis — Novel Mechanism for NMJ Failure

### Hypothesis
Actin-cofilin rods formed via the SMN→PFN→ROCK→LIMK→CFL2 pathway physically
block mRNA granule transport along motor neuron axons. The NMJ — furthest from
the cell body — becomes a "translational desert" where critical synaptic proteins
cannot be locally synthesized, explaining why NMJ fails first in SMA.

### Why This Explains 5 Open Questions in SMA

1. **Why NMJ fails first** — furthest from soma, most dependent on transport
2. **Why proprioceptive synapses fail** — also depend on local translation
3. **Why early SMN restoration works better** — fewer rods, transport still functional
4. **Why ROCK inhibitor + SMN restoration is synergistic** — remove rods + restore SMN
5. **Why exercise helps** — activity promotes actin turnover, potentially clearing rods

### GPT-4o Assessment
- **Novelty**: Moderate-to-High (components known, integration is new)
- **Plausibility**: High (grounded in established biology)
- **Testability**: High (clear experimental readouts)
- **Publishable**: Yes, with fluorescently tagged mRNA + live imaging data

### Key Experiment
SMA motor neuron culture → fluorescently tag mRNA granules → live imaging of
transport before/after Fasudil treatment → measure local translation at NMJ
endpoints using puromycin incorporation (SUnSET assay).

### Platform Hypotheses Added
- H4: "Actin-cofilin rods create translational desert at NMJ" (confidence 0.65)
- H5: "ZPR1 is underexplored SMA modifier" (confidence 0.45)
- Total hypotheses: 1,269

## 12. Fasudil-in-SMA: Feasibility Assessment (Gemini Flash Research)

### Critical Evidence Supporting the Experiment

| Evidence | Source | Significance |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| ROCK1/ROCK2 elevated in SMNΔ7 muscle | Published studies | Pathway IS activated in SMA |
| Phospho-cofilin elevated in SMA | SMA mouse + patient fibroblasts | **Direct confirmation of our hypothesis** |
| Fasudil crosses BBB | Stroke model studies | Can reach spinal motor neurons |
| Fasudil oral bioavailability | Clinical data | Feasible delivery route |
| Phase 2 ALS trial completed | NCT03792490 | Safety data exists |
| Nobody tested Fasudil in SMA | Literature search | **First-in-field opportunity** |

### What This Means

The SMN → PFN1 → ROCK → LIMK → CFL2 → actin rods pathway is NOT just computational
speculation. Three independent lines of evidence support it:
1. ROCK expression is elevated in SMA (muscle data)
2. Phospho-cofilin is elevated in SMA (mouse + patient cells)
3. Actin-cofilin rods form in SMA models (Walter 2021, PMID 33986363)

**The experiment "Fasudil in SMA iPSC-MN → measure phospho-cofilin + TDP-43"
is not only computationally motivated but has biological grounding.**

### Remaining Unknowns
1. ROCK activation in SMA motor neurons specifically (vs. muscle)
2. Fasudil concentration achievable in spinal cord at safe doses
3. Whether cofilin-P reduction in MNs is sufficient for TDP-43 rescue
4. ALS Phase 2 results (NCT03792490) — not yet published, could change assessment

### Proposed Experiment Protocol (for Simon collaboration)
1. SMA iPSC-derived motor neurons (available from Gouti lab, MDC Berlin)
2. Western blot: phospho-cofilin (p-CFL1/2) vs total cofilin → confirm elevation
3. Treatment: Fasudil 10µM, 30µM (48h) — doses from stroke literature
4. Readouts: (a) p-cofilin reduction, (b) actin rod count, (c) TDP-43 nuclear/cytoplasmic ratio
5. Positive control: Y-27632 (another ROCK inhibitor, commonly used)
6. If positive → repeat with MDI-117740 (LIMK inhibitor, more specific)
7. **Total cost estimate: ~$5,000-10,000** (iPSC-MN differentiation + Western + ICC)
8. **Timeline: 6-8 weeks** from cell line to data

## 11. Platform Quality Audit Findings

### Scoring System Calibration Issue
**PLS3 ranks #27 (0.335) — this is wrong.** PLS3 is the strongest non-SMN modifier
with human genetic evidence, animal model validation, and active therapeutic development.
It should be top-5 for non-SMN targets.

Root cause: The scoring formula over-weights `novelty_score` (rewards unknown targets)
and under-weights `evidence_volume` for established targets with hundreds of supporting papers.

**BCL2 ranks #4 (0.452)** — this is also suspicious. BCL2 is broadly relevant to
apoptosis but is NOT a top SMA-specific target.

**Root cause found (prioritizer.py:34-43):**
```
evidence_volume:    0.08  ← too low for established targets
novelty_discovery:  0.15  ← boosts unknown targets (by design)
evidence_quality:   0.18  ← highest weight
```
This is by design ("being well-known shouldn't dominate") — good for discovery,
bad for clinical prioritization. The scoring correctly surfaces novel targets
but incorrectly ranks PLS3 below BCL2.

**Recommended fix:** Create two scoring modes:
1. "Discovery Mode" (current) — novelty-weighted, finds new targets
2. "Clinical Mode" — evidence_volume=0.20, source_independence=0.18, novelty=0.05
Let the user toggle between them on the frontend.
